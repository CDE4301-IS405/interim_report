Abstract

Indoor air pollution has become a critical yet often overlooked environmental and public health concern, particularly in high-density urban regions such as Singapore, where individuals spend up to 90% of their time indoors. Among the various pollutants, volatile organic compounds (VOCs) emitted from common household items like paints, adhesives, furnishings, and cleaning agents, pose significant health risks, ranging from respiratory irritation to carcinogenic effects. Conventional air-cleaning systems, including activated carbon filters and photocatalytic purifiers, are limited by energy consumption, maintenance needs, and by-product formation. This project explores a plant–microbe biofiltration approach as a sustainable, low-energy alternative for VOC removal. Leveraging the synergistic relationship between plants and rhizospheric microorganisms, the system enables continuous biodegradation of VOCs into harmless compounds without secondary waste generation. Through phased research involving plant selection, pilot testing, and proof-of-concept validation, this study investigates the effectiveness of different plant–microbe pairings in reducing concentrations of five key VOCs—formaldehyde, benzene, toluene, ethylbenzene, and xylene (FBTEX). The findings aim to inform the development of a scalable, self-sustaining indoor air purification system that enhances air quality, supports occupant well-being, and aligns with Singapore’s broader sustainability and low-carbon urban design goals.



1. Introduction (Sammi)

1.1 Indoor Air Quality and Pollutants

Air quality is fundamental to human health and well-being, influencing respiratory, cardiovascular, and even cognitive functions (World Health Organization, 2025). While outdoor air pollution has long received attention due to its visible impacts, such as smog, haze, and poor air quality indices, it represents only part of the exposure picture. Indoor air quality (IAQ), by contrast, remains an underappreciated but equally critical aspect of environmental health (Dcarrick, 2024). Indoor pollutants are often invisible, odorless, and difficult to detect without monitoring. (US EPA, 2025).

In the context of Singapore, focusing on indoor air quality is particularly crucial. The country ranks fourth globally among surveyed nations for having indoor air that is worse than outdoor air, underscoring the extent of exposure within enclosed spaces (Air Quality Connected Data Study, 2024). Due to its tropical climate, dense urban landscape, and heavy reliance on air-conditioning, Singaporeans spend up to 90% of their time indoors, breathing an estimated 11,000 litres of air each day (BCA, 2020; Lung Basic, n.d.). Much of this exposure occurs in offices, schools, homes, and shopping malls, where mechanical ventilation systems dominate and pollutant accumulation is common (Pedro et al., 2024c). Understanding the main types and sources of indoor air pollutants is therefore essential to address the underlying causes of poor air quality and its health implications.

Indoor air pollution can be broadly categorized into two main groups: particulate matter and gaseous contaminants:

  1. Particulate Matter (PM)

    PM refers to a complex mixture of microscopic solid particles and liquid droplets suspended in the air. These particles can originate from both outdoor and indoor sources. From the outdoor environment, PM can enter indoor spaces through openings such as doors and windows. Indoor sources include biological materials like pollens, mold spores, and dust mites, while human activities such as cooking or burning candles can further contribute to particulate generation. Prolonged exposure to PM has been associated with several adverse health effects, including increased respiratory symptoms, nonfatal heart attacks, and aggravated asthma (California Air Resources Board, 2015).

  2. Gaseous Contaminants

    Gaseous contaminants consist of both inorganic and organic gases that pose significant health risks in indoor environments. Inorganic gases, such as nitrogen oxides (NOx) and carbon oxides (COx), are reactive gases commonly produced by gas-fueled cooking and heating appliances, as well as cooking stoves, tobacco smoke, and outdoor air infiltration. Prolonged exposure to these gases can lead to respiratory symptoms, fatigue, chest pain, and even reduced brain function and impaired vision (Tran, Park, & Lee, 2020).

On the other hand, organic gases, primarily volatile organic compounds (VOCs), are carbon-based compounds released from numerous indoor sources. These include printers, building materials, and furnishings, as well as household products such as paints, pesticides, dyes, and cleaning agents. Exposure to VOCs can cause eye, nose, and throat irritation, headaches, loss of coordination, and nausea. Long-term exposure may result in damage to the liver, kidney, and nervous system, and has been linked to an increased risk of cancer (Tran, Park, & Lee, 2020).

1.2 Volatile Organic Compounds (VOC) in Air

When comparing the different groups of indoor air pollutants, organic gases such as VOCs exhibit the highest indoor-to-outdoor concentration ratio, highlighting their strong indoor presence (Air Quality Expert Group, 2022). Studies have shown that indoor VOC levels are typically two to five times higher than outdoor concentrations, and may spike even higher in sealed or air-conditioned buildings with poor ventilation (US EPA, 2025). Unlike particulate matter or inorganic gases that may dissipate quickly, VOCs can persist for months or even years because they are continuously emitted from sources such as paints, furniture, adhesives, cleaning agents, and building materials, which is shown to reaching as high as 19 µg/m³ (Zuraimi, Tham, & Sekhar, 2004).

In indoor air, there are a large group of hundreds over different types of VOCs present, which makes it impossible to know specifically on the number of types of VOCs in a certain room as every environment is different. However, these 5 types of VOCs (Formaldehyde, Benzene, Toluene, Ethylbenzene and Xylene (FBTEX)) are among the most frequently-detected and health-relevant indoor VOCs.

Formaldehyde, commonly emitted from building materials, pressed-wood furniture, coatings and adhesives, is a well‐documented irritant to the eyes, nose and throat and has been classified as a probable human carcinogen even at relatively low concentrations (Kaden, Mandin, Nielsen, & Wolkoff, 2010). In large-scale studies across Europe, Asia and North America, indoor formaldehyde concentrations in homes have commonly ranged from about 10 to 40 μg/m³, with upper 95th percentile values above 50 μg/m³ in some case-studies (Canada, 2021).

Meanwhile, the BTEX group are aromatic hydrocarbons produced by solvent use, paints and adhesives, indoor combustion sources and infiltration from vehicle exhaust or attached garages (Vardoulakis et al., 2020). Indoor benzene levels typically span 2 to 12 μg/m³ in non-smoking homes in Europe, with occasional higher peaks associated with smoking or fuel exhaust infiltration (Harrison, Saborit, Dor, & Henderson, 2010).

They are frequently used as indicator compounds for indoor VOC pollution and have been shown to pose both non-carcinogenic (neurological, respiratory) and carcinogenic (e.g., benzene to leukemia) risks in indoor settings. Their high vapour pressure means they readily partition into air, especially in poorly ventilated indoor spaces, leading to concentrations that may significantly exceed outdoor levels (Baberi et al., 2022).

Hence, focus should be given to these 5 types of VOCs; Formaldehyde and BTEX. These VOCs are the most common and harmful indoor VOCs. These pollutants are released from everyday materials present in almost every residence. By studying these five compounds, our project targets the key contributors to poor indoor air quality, helping us develop effective ways to monitor, reduce, and prevent VOC build-up in indoor environments.

1.3 Impacts of VOC

Exposure to VOCs can lead to a broad spectrum of adverse effects.

  1. Health Impacts

    Short-term exposure commonly causes eye, nose and throat irritation, headaches, nausea, and dizziness, while long-term exposure is linked to damage of the liver, kidneys, and central nervous system and increased cancer risk from compounds such as benzene and formaldehyde (Lai, 2025).

  2. Environmental Impacts

    In Singapore, the National Environment Agency (NEA) monitors key VOCs, particularly benzene, toluene, and xylene (BTX), as they react with nitrogen oxides under sunlight to form ground-level ozone and photochemical smog, which degrade air quality and visibility (NEA, 2024).

  3. Productivity and Cognitive Impact

    Beyond health effects, VOC exposure also influences productivity and cognitive performance. A study by Nanyang Technological University (NTU) reported that elevated indoor VOC levels correlate with reduced concentration, memory, and creative task performance, underscoring the direct link between indoor air quality and workplace efficiency (ScienceDaily, 2023).

  4. Economic Impact

    Industry sources indicate that VOC-related emissions from furniture and building materials are linked to reduced workplace productivity, increased sick leave and higher healthcare costs (SFIC, 2025).  According to The Societal Cost of Modifiable Risk Factors in Singapore (2019), air pollution accounted for approximately S$506 million (95% UI S$458–588 million) in combined healthcare and productivity losses (Tan et al., 2023).

Collectively, these findings underscore that VOCs pose not only a public health threat but also a broader environmental and economic concern, with implications that extend beyond individual well-being to national productivity and sustainability objectives. Their contribution to air pollution undermines Singapore’s ongoing efforts to achieve cleaner air, enhance workplace health, and advance its renewable energy and sustainability goals.

Prioritising the reduction of VOC emissions is therefore essential to safeguard both human health and environmental quality. Such efforts are consistent with international health frameworks and Singapore’s local standards, including SS 554:2016 – Code of Practice for Indoor Air Quality, which prescribes guideline limits for total VOCs and formaldehyde in indoor environments (Singapore Standards, n.d.). Given their persistence, prevalence, and well-documented health impacts, VOCs remain a critical target for intervention in Singapore’s strategy to improve indoor air quality and promote safer, healthier living and working spaces.

1.4 Current Solutions to tackle VOC

There are currently strategies used to tackle indoor VOC levels, as shown in Figure 1 below. The pyramid displays 5 different levels of current strategies, which is source control, ventilation, air cleaning, monitoring and behavioural controls. The strategies are ranked based on their effectiveness and sustainability, which follows Singapore Standard SS554:2016 Code of Practice for indoor air quality, prioritising elimination before ventilation, treatment, behavior and control (NEA, n.d.).

Level 01: Source Control
This is the most effective strategy, which involves eliminating or reducing the sources of pollutants. For example, choosing low-VOC paints and building materials, avoiding the use of strong chemical cleaners, and properly maintaining gas appliances to prevent carbon monoxide leaks. This strategy focuses on eliminating pollutant emissions by using low-VOC materials, sealing emission sources or substituting hazardous chemicals. However, its effectiveness depends heavily on accurate identification of emission sources and availability of non-toxic substitutes. In the real environments, many VOCs are continuously released from diverse materials, making complete elimination nearly impossible (US EPA, 2025). Even materials certified as low-VOC may become secondary emission sources under tropical conditions. For example, higher humidity and temperature accelerate off-gassing and result in prolonged re-emissions of VOCs from indoor finishes and furnishings (Yin, Gao, Wei, Wu, & Wang, 2024).

Level 02: Ventilation
This involves increasing the amount of fresh outdoor air coming into an indoor space. This can be as simple as opening windows and doors, or as technical as using mechanical ventilation systems with high-efficiency air filters to bring in clean air and exhaust polluted air. However, its performance is limited in dense urban areas where outdoor air quality can already be compromised by traffic emissions or haze. Increasing ventilation rates also incurs higher energy consumption for cooling and dehumidification, which contradicts sustainability goals and increases operational costs.

Level 04: Monitoring
Monitoring is a preventive-focused strategy that strengthens VOC management by enabling continuous assessment of indoor air quality (IAQ) and early detection of pollutant spikes. Modern VOC monitoring uses sensors that track total VOCs (TVOC), formaldehyde, carbon dioxide, and other co-pollutants in real time, allowing building managers or occupants to intervene before concentrations reach harmful levels. Research shows that integrating IAQ monitoring systems into buildings improves ventilation control, prompt corrective action, and long-term exposure reduction (Marcham, Miller, Springston, Hawkins, & Kollmeyer, 2020). Singapore’s SS 554:2016 Code of Practice for Indoor Air Quality also recommends routine measurement of TVOC and formaldehyde to maintain healthy indoor environments (Singapore Standards, n.d.). With increasing availability of affordable IAQ sensors, monitoring serves as an essential layer of protection, ensuring that VOC levels remain within recommended guidelines and enabling data-driven air-quality management.

Level 05: Behavioural Controls
Behavioral controls address VOC pollution by targeting occupant actions that directly influence emissions, ventilation habits, and exposure duration. Behavioral strategies include choosing low-VOC or fragrance-free products, improving ventilation during pollutant-generating activities, reducing the use of chemical-based cleaners, and avoiding unnecessary indoor combustion. Studies show that behavioural interventions alone can reduce indoor VOC concentrations by 20–60%, depending on the pollutant source and adherence level (Modupe Mewomo, James Olaonipekun Toyin, Comfort Iyiola, & Aluko, 2021). Public awareness campaigns, environmental labeling schemes (e.g., Singapore Green Label), and workplace health programs support these behavioural shifts by guiding occupants toward safer product choices and good IAQ practices. As the top tier of the mitigation hierarchy, behavioural controls reinforce long-term sustainability by addressing VOC production at its behavioural roots.

While monitoring and behavioural controls are essential components of a comprehensive indoor air-quality strategy, monitoring systems, although valuable, only detect and inform users about VOC concentrations. They do not actively reduce pollutant loads. Similarly, behavioural controls depend on consistent user compliance, which is often difficult to sustain in real-world settings such as offices, homes, and public buildings where occupants have varying awareness and habits. Given these behavioural and operational constraints, it is necessary to evaluate whether lower-tier measures, source control and ventilation, can sufficiently maintain safe VOC levels.

Current IAQ practice prioritises source control and adequate ventilation, which can significantly lower pollutant loads. Source control and ventilation reduce VOC levels by ~55%. However, they are unable to eliminate them fully due to the continuous emissions from indoor sources (Joo et al., 2025). A meta-analysis of 77 residential IAQ studies found multiple VOCs still exceeding chronic health benchmarks despite typical ventilation rates, indicating that source control and dilution alone are insufficient, thus calling for focus on active air-cleaning measures (Logue et al., 2011; Mata et al., 2022).

Level 03: Air Cleaning
Air-cleaning strategies involve using mechanical or chemical devices to remove pollutants from indoor air. For particulate matter such as PM₂.₅, High-Efficiency Particulate Air (HEPA) filters are highly effective because they capture up to 99.97% of particles ≥0.3 μm. However, HEPA filters are unable to remove VOCs, as gaseous molecules pass through filter fibres (US EPA, 2022). For VOCs, air purifiers typically rely on activated carbon or other sorbent filters, which adsorb gaseous pollutants but do not destroy them. Once saturated, these filters can re-release VOCs back into the room, making frequent replacement necessary and generating additional waste (ScienceDirect, 2023). Other technologies, such as photocatalytic oxidation (PCO) and UV-based oxidation systems, attempt to chemically degrade VOCs but often face issues of incomplete oxidation, which can lead to the formation of harmful secondary pollutants (Zhong, Haghighat, & Lee, 2013). PCO units also require continuous high-energy input, suffer from catalyst degradation, and may release nanoparticles such as TiO₂ during prolonged operation (R. Sivasankar et al., 2018). These limitations make conventional air-cleaning less suitable for long-term, low-cost VOC management in typical residential or community settings.

Conventional air-cleaning technologies, including HEPA filters, catalytic oxidation and PCO devices, therefore offer targeted but incomplete VOC removal. Their performance is constrained by narrow pollutant specificity, energy demand, maintenance requirements, and environmental trade-offs.

Studies have repeatedly shown that VOC removal efficiencies for activated carbon, PCO, and plasma purifiers vary widely, with many systems achieving only modest or inconsistent reductions under real indoor conditions (Messan , 2022). In addition, the potential formation of secondary pollutants, particularly aldehydes and ultrafine particles, raises concerns about the long-term safety of oxidation-based technologies (R. Sivasankar et al., 2018). Together, these persistent limitations of conventional air-cleaning devices, especially their high energy demand, incomplete degradation, risk of harmful by-products, and filter-dependent maintenance cycles, underscore the need for safer and more sustainable VOC mitigation solutions.

This highlights the need for scalable, sustainable, and nature-based alternatives. Plant–microbe biofiltration systems address these gaps by providing continuous, low-cost, and biologically driven VOC removal, making them a promising next-generation solution for improving indoor air quality in Singapore’s enclosed and air-conditioned environments.

Plant–microbe systems offer broad-spectrum VOC removal, low energy consumption, and negligible by-product risks. These systems metabolise VOCs into harmless end products through biological pathways rather than adsorption or incomplete oxidation. This biological synergy expands the range of VOCs that can be removed, including BTEX compounds, alcohols, aldehydes, and oxygenated VOCs (Sheoran et al., 2022). Because plant–microbe systems operate passively or with minor airflow assistance, they require minimal energy input and avoid the maintenance burden associated with filter replacement or catalyst regeneration. Their ability to self-regenerate, adapt to pollutant loads, and benefit indoor occupants through biophilic effects further strengthens their suitability as a long-term IAQ strategy.

1.5 Plant-Microbe System

Plants are increasingly recognized as a sustainable and effective means of mitigating VOCs in indoor and urban environments through a process known as phytoremediation, as shown in Figure 2. This process harnesses the ability of plants and their associated root microorganisms to capture, transform, and degrade gaseous pollutants into less harmful substances. VOC uptake occurs primarily through leaf stomata and cuticular adsorption, where compounds such as formaldehyde, benzene, and toluene are absorbed and subsequently metabolized within plant tissues through oxidation, conjugation, and sequestration processes (Wolverton & Nelson, 2020). Some VOCs are translocated via the transpiration stream to roots, where rhizospheric microorganisms further degrade them enzymatically into benign end-products such as CO₂, water, and organic acids (Green Plants for Green Buildings, 2014).

From Figure 3, research has shown that plants hosting active microbial communities remove significantly higher VOC concentrations compared to sterilized systems, demonstrating that plants and microbes act collaboratively to purify air (Ravindra & Mor, 2022).

Microbes play a crucial role in enhancing the ability of plants to purify air because they act as the biochemical engines behind pollutant breakdown. While plants absorb VOCs through their leaves and roots, most of actual degradation happens through the microorganisms living around the rhizosphere and on leaf surfaces. These microbes, which are mainly bacteria and fungi, use VOCs as a carbon and energy source, metabolizing them via enzymatic reactions such as monooxygenation and dioxygenation, which transform harmful compounds like BTEX into CO₂, water and organic acids (Ravindra & Mor, 2022).

The rhizosphere, a narrow zone of soil influenced by root exudates, serves as a hotspot for microbial activity, facilitating synergistic plant–microbe interactions that enhance VOC degradation rates. Plant roots exude sugars, amino acids and other organic compounds which feed the microbial communities, helping them thrive and maintain high enzymatic activities. In return, these microbes break down VOCs that have diffused into soil or root zone, preventing them from releasing into the air again. Moreover, when plants are exposed to certain VOCs, their microbial partners can adapt and evolve, which enriches VOC-degrading species such as Pseudomonas and Rhodanobacter, which increase the overall capacity of the system for pollutant breakdown (Cruz et al., 2023). Research has shown that this symbiotic mechanism can significantly reduce concentrations of formaldehyde, benzene, and xylene in enclosed spaces, with removal efficiencies exceeding 60% under optimized airflow and plant density conditions (Irga, Pettit, & Torpy, 2018).

Beyond pollutant removal, phytoremediation contributes additional co-benefits such as humidity regulation, oxygen generation, and psychological well-being, making it a multifunctional and eco-friendly approach for improving indoor air quality.

1.6 Current Plant-Microbe Methods in Market

In the current market, there are 3 plant-microbe methods researched or already implemented to tackle indoor air pollutants, which is Engineered Microbial Inoculation, Photocatalytic Biofiltration and Smart Green Wall.

  1. Engineered Microbial Inoculation

    Recent biotechnology start-ups such as Neoplants have introduced systems that integrate engineered plant–microbe pairs to enhance indoor VOC degradation. These genetically modified microbes are designed to express specific enzymes capable of breaking down certain types of common pollutants more efficiently than natural microbial communities. When paired with plants, these microbes accelerate root-zone microbial biodegradation. It is shown that this use of this system has 30 % lower VOC (BTX) concentrations than control after 13 days of continuous emission (NEOPLANTS, n.d).

    However, the key drawback lies in biosafety and containment concerns. Current product information provides limited detail on how these engineered microorganisms are contained within indoor environments or prevented from escaping into natural ecosystems. Risks include potential horizontal gene transfer to other microbes and uncontrolled proliferation outside intended environments. These can lead to the disruption to the ecological system due to potential competition with native soil or indoor microbes, which will alter microbial community balance and nutrient cycles. Engineered microbes might spread to unrelated environmental bacteria, potentially giving them unnatural metabolic capabilities or changing ecological functions (OECD, n.d). Without transparent biosafety data or regulatory oversight, there is uncertainty regarding the long-term ecological impact of these engineered systems, which poses a significant barrier to widespread adoption in public or commercial buildings.

  2. Photocatalytic Biofiltration

    Another emerging solution, used in products such as NATEDE Smart Air Purifiers, combines natural biofiltration with photocatalytic oxidation (PCO) to degrade VOCs. With reference to Figure 4, this hybrid approach uses a TiO₂ or WO3 -based catalyst activated by visible or UV light to produce reactive oxygen species that oxidize VOCs into simpler compounds. A small fan unit is installed beneath the entire system to circulate cleaned air outwards to the atmosphere. The integration of plant biofiltration with photocatalysis allows for faster breakdown of pollutants and reduced need for mechanical filters. It is shown that more than 75% of VOCs, such as methyl, ethyl, ketone (MEK) are removed in 24 hours, while plant in standard pot is only 11%. Moreover, there is a higher removal efficiency of 85% of formaldehyde after 1 hour of using the photocatalytic system (NATEDE, 2024).

    Nevertheless, PCO systems face two key limitations. First, incomplete oxidation can result in the formation of harmful secondary by-products such as formaldehyde and acetaldehyde, which compromise indoor air safety rather than improve it (Mo et al., 2019). Research has shown that incomplete oxidation is actually common under realistic indoor conditions, with by-products yield of an estimated 15% in a moderate removal efficiency (Yu et al., 2021). Second, prolonged operation leads to catalyst degradation and nanoparticle release from coatings such as TiO₂ or WO₃, reducing efficiency and potentially posing inhalation hazards (Koivisto et al., 2018). These issues highlight that while photocatalytic biofilters are innovative, their long-term safety and durability in real indoor environments remain uncertain.

  3. Smart Green Wall

    Commercial systems such as the NAAVA Smart Green Wall, as shown in Figure 5, utilize natural plant–microbe biofiltration in an active airflow setup. Air is drawn through a soilless growth medium containing plant roots and associated microorganisms, where VOCs such as MEK and toluene are absorbed and enzymatically degraded. Studies have shown that these systems can achieve VOC removal efficiencies of 50–70% in single-pass tests (Naava, n.d.).

    However, their primary drawback is spatial and architectural constraint. Each wall unit requires substantial vertical space, a dedicated water and airflow system, and periodic maintenance, which can limit deployment in compact offices, classrooms, or high-density residential units. The size of one NAAVA wall is approximately 1003(W) x 2113(H) x 350(D) mm, which takes up about the same footprint as a built-in wardrobe in residences (Naava, n.d.). Its large space use makes it challenging to integrate in smaller interiors, such as residences and small office areas where floor or wall space is limited. Consequently, despite their proven purification potential, scalability and adaptability to diverse indoor layouts remain restricted.

While existing plant–microbe biofiltration systems demonstrate promising VOC-removal capabilities, each of them faces critical implementation barriers as shown in Figure 6, which highlight the need for a more practical and accessible biological air purification system suitable for household and office environments, with the safety, compactness, and adaptability required for modern urban interiors.

2. Project Overview (Pearlyn)

2.1. Research Question

Plant–microbe systems represent an advanced extension of phytoremediation, harnessing the synergistic relationship between plants and their root-associated microorganisms to enhance VOC degradation efficiency (Pliushch & Pliushch, 2023). Despite this potential, existing implementations remain constrained by limitations in safety, scalability, and practicality, underscoring the need for systems that are durable, low-maintenance, and easily adoptable in real-world settings (Aydogan, 2020).

Building upon these insights, this project seeks to explore the potential of developing a safe, practical and accessible biological air purification system suitable for household environments. Specifically, the study aims to address the research question:

“Which commercially available indoor plant and microbe pairing demonstrates the greatest VOC reduction efficiency among the five most common indoor VOCs (Formaldehyde, Benzene, Toluene, Ethylbenzene, and Xylene)?”

By investigating this question, the project aims to identify an optimal plant–microbe combination that offers a sustainable, low-cost, and effective solution for improving indoor air quality in everyday living spaces.

2.2. Project Phases

To systematically address the research question, the study was structured into 5 sequential phases, as illustrated in Figure 7. Each phase builds upon the findings of the previous one to progressively narrow down the most effective plant–microbe pairing for VOC removal. The overall research framework integrates plant screening, pilot testing, proof of concept validation, microbial identification, and final pairing evaluation to ensure a comprehensive and evidence-based approach.



3. Phase 1: Plant Selection (Pearlyn)

The first phase of the project focuses on identifying indoor plant species that can most effectively remove VOCs from enclosed environments. To achieve this, a systematic research approach was adopted to shortlist suitable plants for subsequent experimentation.

The plant selection process began with an industrial review to identify species currently used in commercially available plant-based air purification systems. These industry findings provided practical insights into plant performance under real-world indoor conditions. Building upon this foundation, the review was expanded to include secondary research from academic and organizational studies, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of documented VOC removal capabilities across a wide range of species.

From this combined body of research, a comparative analysis was conducted to evaluate reported VOC removal efficiencies. Plant species that consistently demonstrated lower performance or limited removal capability were excluded, refining the pool to those with higher and more reliable purification potential.

Following this performance-based screening, the remaining candidates were further evaluated according to a defined set of selection criteria — availability, suitability to Singapore’s indoor climate, and ease of maintenance. These criteria ensure that the shortlisted species are not only effective in VOC removal but also practical and sustainable for long-term indoor application. The following diagram (Figure 8) summarizes this selection framework.

Based on the established selection criteria, four plant species were shortlisted for subsequent testing in the pilot study and proof-of-concept phases (Phase 2 and Phase 3): Sansevieria trifasciata (Snake Plant), Epipremnum aureum (Money Plant), Zamioculcas zamiifolia (ZZ Plant), and Dracaena fragrans (Dragon Plant).

The selection of these species was guided by two primary considerations. First, consistent VOC removal performance — these plants were repeatedly identified in both scientific literature and commercial reports as effective species for VOC mitigation (see Appendix 1), thereby supporting their inclusion based on validated evidence. Second, ease of maintenance and resilience — the shortlisted plants are hardy, low-maintenance species capable of thriving under typical indoor environmental conditions. Their adaptability to Lightweight Expanded Clay Aggregate (LECA) as a growth medium further reinforces their practicality for real-world applications.

These combined characteristics make the selected species well-suited for the pilot study and proof-of-concept phases, where the primary objective is to validate the feasibility, robustness, and reliability of the experimental setup under simulated indoor conditions.


4. Phase 2: Pilot Study (Pearlyn)

Phase 2 focuses on validating the feasibility of our experimental setup and reliability of our method of analysis through a pilot study. This phase serves as a crucial bridge between preliminary plant screening and subsequent microbial investigations, ensuring that our experimental framework is both robust and scientifically sound before advancing to larger-scale testing.

A pilot study was conducted within the chamber over 24-hour test cycles to assess and refine the experimental setup. The objectives of this study are:
  1. To evaluate the feasibility of the experimental design
  2. To determine the appropriate amount of pollutants to be used
  3. To establish the optimal duration for each experimental run
  4. To identify any potential issues or gaps in the setup that may affect the accuracy or reliability of subsequent experiments.

By achieving these objectives, the pilot study generates critical insights that inform subsequent refinements to the experimental setup and strengthen the reliability of data analysis in later phases. The pilot study adopts a cyclical experimental framework (Figure 10), beginning with the definition of key parameters such as pollutant dosage. Experiments are then conducted and systematically evaluated to identify gaps, inconsistencies, or methodological limitations. Based on these observations, necessary adjustments are implemented, and the process is repeated until optimal experimental conditions are established — where pollutant concentrations fall within the sensor’s detection range while remaining sufficiently high to allow discernible differences in VOC removal efficiency.

Elmer’s White Glue was selected as a primary pollutant source because it contains all five target VOCs, enabling simultaneous assessment of multiple compounds within a single material. However, its high viscosity restricts solvent mobility and diffusion, leading to slower VOC evaporation rates. Such behaviour is typical of viscous polymeric coatings and adhesives, where internal mass transfer resistance limits emission flux (Munekata et al., 2013). If only white glue were used, the slower VOC emission would likely prolong the time required for concentrations in the chamber to reach a plateau, potentially delaying the observation of measurable VOC removal by the plant and plant–microbe setups. To address this limitation and ensure sufficient VOC exposure during the experimental period, additional pollutant sources with differing physical properties were incorporated.

Aureo’s Liquid Shoe Polish was incorporated as a complementary VOC source due to its low viscosity, which facilitates more rapid evaporation compared with white glue. This characteristic ensures that the chamber reaches measurable VOC concentrations more quickly, enabling timely observation of VOC removal by the plant and plant–microbe setups. In addition, the shoe polish provides a concentrated source of Toluene, Ethylbenzene, and Xylene, broadening the spectrum of VOCs present and reflecting the diverse chemical profile commonly found in indoor environments.

Nippon Vinilex‑5000 water-based paint was added to supplement the Formaldehyde content, which is released more slowly from high-viscosity white glue. Even though it is a low-VOC formulation, paint contributes an additional, readily available source of Formaldehyde, helping to balance the relative concentrations of all five target VOCs in the chamber. The inclusion of paint also strengthens the experimental design by preventing reliance on a single pollutant type, reducing potential bias due to the physical and chemical properties unique to one material. This approach ensures that the experimental conditions more accurately mimic real-world indoor settings, where VOCs originate from multiple sources such as adhesives, paints, and personal care products.

In practice, VOC volatilization depends on factors such as viscosity, diffusion, and available surface area, meaning that complete evaporation may not occur. Therefore, 2.5 mL was selected as a practical and conservative starting volume to ensure that VOC concentrations in the chamber were sufficiently high to detect and compare the removal performance of the plant and plant–microbe setups.

4.1.2 Plant Container and Airflow Design

The plant container was prepared to ensure consistent experimental conditions and to facilitate effective plant–microbe interaction for VOC removal. To optimize pollutant exposure to the plant roots, the container was modified by cutting an opening and installing a computer fan, which directs airflow from the pollutant source toward the LECA and root zone (Figure 13). This arrangement ensures that VOCs pass through the rhizosphere, maximizing contact with both the plant and microbial components.

LECA balls were carefully washed prior to use to remove any surface contaminants that could interfere with the results. The container was filled with LECA to approximately 80% of its volume, maintaining uniform growth medium across all setups (Figure 14).

A volume of 250 mL of water was added to each container to ensure consistent hydration, and the same volume of microbial solution was applied to the plant–microbe setups by carefully pouring it around the root zone. This ensured that the microbes were evenly distributed across the entire root system, allowing them to effectively colonize the rhizosphere and enhance VOC degradation by metabolizing compounds absorbed by the roots, thereby improving overall removal efficiency (Figure 14).

4.1.3 Sensor Calibration

Accurate measurement of total volatile organic compounds (TVOCs) is essential for evaluating the effectiveness of each plant–microbe pairing. To ensure reliable data collection, the Awair Element air quality monitor was selected for use in this study. The Awair Element is recognized as one of the most accurate and reliable commercially available indoor air-quality sensors, capable of detecting a wide range of pollutants including TVOCs, CO₂, and particulate matter (Shipped F, n.d.).

Calibration of the sensors was conducted under outdoor ambient conditions for a continuous 24-hour period to establish a stable and accurate baseline. This baseline provides a reference for detecting changes in VOC levels within the enclosed chamber during subsequent experiments. To enhance calibration consistency, two Awair Element sensors were placed facing each other during the calibration process as shown in Figure 15. This arrangement ensured that both devices sampled the same air mass, minimizing discrepancies due to airflow direction or localized variations in pollutant concentration.

Following outdoor calibration, the sensors were allowed to stabilize briefly under indoor conditions before each experimental run. This step ensured that all sensors started from comparable baseline readings, improving measurement precision and repeatability across tests.

4.1.4 Chamber Modification

In designing the experimental chamber, several factors were considered to ensure appropriate plant growth and accurate VOC measurements.

The chamber size was selected based on several practical and methodological considerations. First, the height of the chamber was chosen to accommodate the combined height of the plant and its container, ensuring that the plant remained upright and unobstructed during the experiment. Second, the base area of the chamber had to be sufficiently large to position both the pollutant source and the sensor far apart, minimizing the risk of the sensor detecting artificially elevated VOC levels from direct release rather than from the ambient chamber environment. At the same time, the chamber could not be excessively large, as a greater internal volume would dilute the VOC concentration and prolong the time needed to reach measurable steady-state levels. Therefore, a moderate-sized transparent chamber (H: 56 cm × B: 39 cm × L: 42 cm) was chosen to balance spatial requirements, measurement accuracy, and experimental efficiency.

A transparent plastic chamber was also selected to simulate indoor conditions, providing natural light to the plants while avoiding complete darkness, which could adversely affect plant metabolism and VOC uptake. Transparency also facilitated visual monitoring of plant health throughout the experiment.

To accommodate instrumentation, a small orifice was drilled at the base of the chamber (Figure 16), allowing the computer fan and sensors to connect to external power sources while minimizing leakage of the sealed air. This setup ensures the chamber remains effectively enclosed, so that any observed changes in VOC concentration can be attributed to the plant or plant–microbe system rather than external influences.

4.1.5 Final Experimental Setup

Building on the preparation of the individual components, the experimental setup was configured to ensure controlled and consistent measurement of VOC removal. The plant container was positioned such that the computer fan directed airflow from the pollutant source toward the plant roots, while the sensor was placed on the opposite side of the chamber to minimize direct exposure to emitted VOCs (Figure 17).

The chamber was also sealed using masking tape and Blu Tack to create a fully enclosed environment, ensuring that any observed reduction in VOC concentration could be attributed solely to the plant or plant–microbe system (Figure 18).

4.3. Experimental Improvements and Methodology Refinement

During this process, several important considerations for improving the setup and methodology of analysis were identified, including adjustments to pollutant volume, accounting for background VOCs emitted by the chamber material, and ensuring consistent sensor calibration and placement.

Firstly, when 2.5 mL of each pollutant was introduced into the chamber, the TVOC levels reached 37,216.6 ppb, which exceeded the sensor’s upper limit of 36,000 ppb (AWAIR support, 2023). After varying pollutant volumes, we determined that using 1 mL of each pollutant maintained the TVOC concentration within the sensor’s detectable range (29,103.4 ppb) while still producing a clear difference in VOC removal between setups (Figure 20). Hence, 1 mL of each pollutant was selected as the standardized dosage for subsequent experiments.

Figure 20: VOC Concentration Over Time for 1ml of each pollutant- Interactive visualization

Secondly, a control test conducted using an empty chamber showed a gradual increase in TVOC levels even in the absence of added pollutants (Figure 19 - 'empty'). This is likely due to the emission of VOCs from the polypropylene chamber material, which can occur when the plastic is exposed to ultraviolet light. It is important to note that the VOCs released from polypropylene are not among the FBTEX compounds targeted in this study (Kang et al., 2020). However, because the sensors used measure total VOC (TVOC) concentration without differentiating between compound types, these background emissions may still influence the readings. To minimize this effect, each experimental condition will be repeated twice, and the average values will be used for analysis. Since these background VOCs are not chemically related to the FBTEX compounds of interest, they are not expected to affect the comparative assessment of plant–microbe pairings.

Lastly, we observed slight differences in the starting VOC levels across different setups. These variations are likely caused by differences in the time taken to seal the chamber or by residual VOCs that remained despite ventilation between runs. As a result, direct comparison of absolute VOC readings between experiments may not provide an accurate representation of removal performance. To ensure fair comparison, all future analyses will instead be based on the relative change in VOC concentration from the starting point of each experiment.

Appendix 2: Calculation of Amount of Pollutant

Based on Natede (2023), who used 30 cm³ of pollutant in a 33 m³ chamber, the corresponding pollutant volume per cubic metre is approximately 0.909 cm³/m³. For the experimental chamber in this study (0.0917 m³; 56 cm × 39 cm × 42 cm), the equivalent pollutant volume is:


0.0917 m³ × 0.909 cm³/m³ ≈ 0.083 cm³


To convert the calculated pollutant volume into the corresponding mass for each target VOC, the density of each compound must be considered (Table 6).

Table 6: Densities of FBTEX compounds
Compound Density (g/cm³)
Formaldehyde 0.8153
Benzene 0.8765
Toluene 0.8623
Ethylbenzene 0.867
Xylene (mixed isomers) ~ 0.86–0.88

Assuming equal composition of the different compounds, the average density is used for simplicity in calculations (approximately 0.856 g/cm³). The mass of the pollutant required can be calculated using the standard relation:


Mass (g) = Volume (cm³) × Density (g/cm³)
Mass ≈ 0.083 cm³ × 0.856 g/cm³ ≈ 0.071 g


Table 7 summarizes the densities, VOC content, and corresponding mass of VOC per unit volume for each pollutant used in the pilot study. By multiplying the density by the VOC fraction, the effective mass of VOCs per unit volume of each pollutant was calculated.
