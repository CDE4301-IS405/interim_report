1.1 Indoor Air Quality and Pollutants

Air quality is fundamental to human health and well-being. The air we breathe influences a wide range of physiological functions, including respiratory, cardiovascular, and cognitive systems (World Health Organisation, n.d.). Traditionally, the focus has been on outdoor air pollution, since its effects are visible through smog, haze, and poor air indices, and it has been established as a major global health risk. Indoor air quality remains a neglected aspect of environmental health (Dcarrick, 2025). Unlike outdoor air pollution, which is visible through haze or measurable by public indices, indoor pollutants are often invisible and odorless, making them harder to detect and easier to overlook.

2. Project Overview (Pearlyn)

2.1. Research Question

Plant–microbe systems represent an advanced extension of phytoremediation, harnessing the synergistic relationship between plants and their root-associated microorganisms to enhance VOC degradation efficiency (Pliushch & Pliushch, 2023). Despite this potential, existing implementations remain constrained by limitations in safety, scalability, and practicality, underscoring the need for systems that are durable, low-maintenance, and easily adoptable in real-world settings (Aydogan, 2020).

Building upon these insights, this project seeks to explore the potential of developing a safe, practical and accessible biological air purification system suitable for household environments. Specifically, the study aims to address the research question:

“Which commercially available indoor plant and microbe pairing demonstrates the greatest VOC reduction efficiency among the five most common indoor VOCs (Formaldehyde, Benzene, Toluene, Ethylbenzene, and Xylene)?”

By investigating this question, the project aims to identify an optimal plant–microbe combination that offers a sustainable, low-cost, and effective solution for improving indoor air quality in everyday living spaces.

2.2. Project Phases

To systematically address the research question, the study was structured into 5 sequential phases, as illustrated in Figure 6. Each phase builds upon the findings of the previous one to progressively narrow down the most effective plant–microbe pairing for VOC removal. The overall research framework integrates plant screening, pilot testing, proof of concept validation, microbial identification, and final pairing evaluation to ensure a comprehensive and evidence-based approach.



3. Phase 1: Plant Selection (Pearlyn)

The first phase of the project focuses on identifying indoor plant species that can most effectively remove VOCs from enclosed environments. To achieve this, a systematic research approach was adopted to shortlist suitable plants for subsequent experimentation.

The plant selection process began with an industrial review to identify species currently used in commercially available plant-based air purification systems. These industry findings provided practical insights into plant performance under real-world indoor conditions. Building upon this foundation, the review was expanded to include secondary research from academic and organizational studies, ensuring a comprehensive understanding of documented VOC removal capabilities across a wide range of species.

From this combined body of research, a comparative analysis was conducted to evaluate reported VOC removal efficiencies. Plant species that consistently demonstrated lower performance or limited removal capability were excluded, refining the pool to those with higher and more reliable purification potential.

Following this performance-based screening, the remaining candidates were further evaluated according to a defined set of selection criteria — availability, suitability to Singapore’s indoor climate, and ease of maintenance. These criteria ensure that the shortlisted species are not only effective in VOC removal but also practical and sustainable for long-term indoor application. The following diagram (Figure 7) summarizes this selection framework.

This multi-stage selection process ensured that the final list of candidate plants not only demonstrated scientific potential for VOC removal, but also satisfied practical considerations for real-world implementation in residential and commercial indoor spaces.

Based on the established selection criteria, four plant species were shortlisted for subsequent testing in the pilot study and proof-of-concept phases (Phase 2 and Phase 3): Sansevieria trifasciata (Snake Plant), Epipremnum aureum (Money Plant), Zamioculcas zamiifolia (ZZ Plant), and Dracaena fragrans (Dragon Plant).



4. Phase 2: Pilot Study (Pearlyn)

Phase 2 focuses on validating the feasibility of our experimental setup and reliability of our method of analysis through a pilot study. This phase serves as a crucial bridge between preliminary plant screening and subsequent microbial investigations, ensuring that our experimental framework is both robust and scientifically sound before advancing to larger-scale testing.

A pilot study was conducted within the chamber over 24-hour test cycles to assess and refine the experimental setup. The objectives of this study are:
  1. To evaluate the feasibility of the experimental design
  2. To determine the appropriate amount of pollutants to be used
  3. To establish the optimal duration for each experimental run
  4. To identify any potential issues or gaps in the setup that may affect the accuracy or reliability of subsequent experiments.

By achieving these objectives, the pilot study generates critical insights that inform subsequent refinements to the experimental setup and strengthen the reliability of data analysis in later phases. The pilot study adopts a cyclical experimental framework (Figure 9), beginning with the definition of key parameters such as pollutant dosage. Experiments are then conducted and systematically evaluated to identify gaps, inconsistencies, or methodological limitations. Based on these observations, necessary adjustments are implemented, and the process is repeated until optimal experimental conditions are established — where pollutant concentrations fall within the sensor’s detection range while remaining sufficiently high to allow discernible differences in VOC removal efficiency.

Secondly, a control test conducted using an empty chamber showed a gradual increase in TVOC levels even in the absence of added pollutants. This is likely due to the emission of VOCs from the polypropylene chamber material, which can occur when the plastic is exposed to ultraviolet light. It is important to note that the VOCs released from polypropylene are not among the FBTEX compounds targeted in this study (Kang et al., 2020). However, because the sensors used measure total VOC (TVOC) concentration without differentiating between compound types, these background emissions may still influence the readings. To minimize this effect, each experimental condition will be repeated multiple times, and the average values will be used for analysis. Since these background VOCs are not chemically related to the FBTEX compounds of interest, they are not expected to affect the comparative assessment of plant–microbe pairings. Lastly, we observed slight differences in the starting VOC levels across different setups. These variations are likely caused by differences in the time taken to seal the chamber or by residual VOCs that remained despite ventilation between runs. As a result, direct comparison of absolute VOC readings between experiments may not provide an accurate representation of removal performance. To ensure fair comparison, all future analyses will instead be based on the relative change in VOC concentration from the starting point of each experiment.
